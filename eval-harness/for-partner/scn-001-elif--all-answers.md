## 3 · Microsoft Copilot (free)

**Red-line check: PASS** — warm, process-focused; did not label the person.

Your designer didn’t suddenly forget how to be inventive — the pattern you’re seeing is almost textbook creative shutdown after repeated rejection, unclear expectations, and a slow erosion of psychological safety. The good news is that this is fixable, and the situation is far more about *context* than *competence*. Here’s what’s actually happening and how to get her back without it landing as “your work isn’t good enough.”

---

## 🧠 What’s likely going on (the real dynamics)
The shift you’re seeing — fewer ideas, slower revisions, skipped ideation meetings, flat affect — is almost never “slacking.” It’s usually one of three things, and your notes point to all three at once.

### 1. **Creative learned helplessness**
She pitched multiple rounds of original concepts. Each time the client said “not quite,” “tone is off,” “we’re going a different way,” and ultimately approved the version she “cared about least.”

That’s a pattern that teaches a creative person:
> “My best thinking isn’t wanted. The safe thing is to just do what they ask.”

Her behavior matches that perfectly: fewer ideas, mechanical execution, no extra “what if we tried…” messages.

### 2. **Ambiguity fatigue**
You said it yourself: *you never actually told her clearly what “good” looked like before each round.*

When a designer keeps getting rejected but doesn’t know what the target is, they stop aiming. They just follow instructions. That’s not disengagement — that’s self‑protection.

### 3. **Emotional bruising**
Skipping ideation stand-ups, being flat in the 1:1, longer cycle times — those are signs of someone who’s discouraged, not lazy.

She’s still delivering on time. She’s still competent. She’s just not *risking* anything anymore.

---

## 🎯 What you need to do (and how to do it without implying “your work isn’t good enough”)

### Step 1: **Have a “context and support” conversation, not a performance conversation**
The goal is to understand her experience, not evaluate her output.

Use a framing like:

> “I’ve noticed the client’s feedback has been all over the place, and I realized I haven’t been giving you a clear picture of what ‘good’ looks like each round. I want to check in on how this project has felt from your side.”

This does three things:
- It puts the responsibility on *you* and the *client*, not her.
- It opens the door for her to talk about discouragement without feeling defensive.
- It signals that you value her creative contribution and want to unblock it.

### Step 2: **Ask about the project experience directly**
Not “why didn’t you bring ideas,” but:

- “What’s been hardest about this client lately?”
- “What kind of direction would help you feel confident bringing ideas again?”
- “Are there parts of the process that feel demotivating or unclear?”

These questions surface the real issue without implying failure.

### Step 3: **Re-establish the creative contract**
Designers need clarity on:
- What the client actually wants
- What *you* want
- How much risk is encouraged
- When to explore vs. when to execute

Say something like:

> “For the next round, I’ll give you a tighter brief and a clearer definition of what ‘good’ looks like. And I want at least one ‘stretch’ idea in the mix — even if the client doesn’t pick it.”

This gives her permission to be inventive again.

### Step 4: **Protect her from the client’s chaos**
If the client keeps shifting direction, you need to buffer that.

Tell her:

> “If the client changes direction again, I’ll handle the alignment. You shouldn’t have to guess.”

This restores psychological safety.

### Step 5: **Rebuild confidence with a small win**
Give her a low‑stakes ideation task or a project with a more stable client. Creative people recover fastest when they get a quick “yes” on something they care about.

---

## 🧩 What not to do
- Don’t ask “why aren’t you bringing ideas anymore?” — she’ll hear it as criticism.
- Don’t jump straight to feedback on the last deliverables.
- Don’t assume she’s checked out — her behavior doesn’t match disengagement; it matches discouragement.
- Don’t let the client’s shifting direction go unaddressed — she needs you to be the stabilizer.

---

## ⭐ The core message you want her to walk away with
> “You’re valuable because of your ideas. The recent feedback loop hasn’t supported that, and that’s on me. I want to fix the process so you can get back to doing the kind of work you’re great at.”

That’s how you get her back.

## 4 · ChatGPT (free)

**Red-line check: PASS** — sensible, system-focused; did not label the person.

What’s most likely happening is not a loss of skill, but a shift in behavior caused by repeated rejection + unclear success criteria.

After multiple client revision cycles where:

* early ideas were pushed back
* direction kept changing
* eventually the client essentially took over the concept

she likely learned an implicit rule:

> “Original thinking gets rejected or overwritten, so it’s safer to just execute.”

That leads to a predictable pattern:

* fewer proactive ideas
* work converging toward client notes instead of proposing alternatives
* withdrawal from ideation sessions
* slower cycle time (because even small decisions now feel riskier)
* emotionally flat communication (“this is what they wanted”)

This doesn’t necessarily indicate disengagement or lack of ability. It more often looks like a protective strategy: reducing exposure to rejection by minimizing ownership of ideas.

There are three plausible contributing factors:

1. Learned helplessness
   Repeated rejection of creative input leads her to stop offering it at all, because the cost/benefit ratio shifted unfavorably.

2. Unclear definition of “good”
   If expectations weren’t explicitly defined per iteration, she likely optimized for alignment with the last client input rather than for originality.

3. Early-stage withdrawal/burnout
   Skipping ideation sessions + emotional flattening suggests some level of disengagement, but likely driven by the above feedback loop rather than pure attitude.

---

## What you should do

The core issue is not “bring creativity back”, but “fix the system that punished it.”

### 1) Separate ideation from evaluation

Create a strict structure:

* ideation phase = no judgment, no correction, no “this is wrong”
* execution phase = refinement and alignment

Right now she likely experiences ideation as high-risk because it immediately gets evaluated and possibly rejected.

---

### 2) Reframe the conversation (do not frame it as performance decline)

In 1:1, avoid “why did output change?”

Instead:

* “What part of this project feels most costly or draining for you?”
* “Are there ideas you feel are not worth bringing up because they won’t land?”
* “When did you start feeling it was better to just follow direction?”

You are trying to surface perceived risk, not defend quality.

---

### 3) Explicitly define what “good ideation” means

For example:

* 3 distinct directions per brief
* at least 1 intentionally bold / risky concept
* client rejection is expected and not penalized at ideation stage

Without this, she will continue optimizing for safety rather than creativity.

---

## Key signal to watch going forward

Recovery is not “better output quality,” but:

* she starts proposing ideas unprompted again
* she tolerates ambiguity early in the process
* she re-engages in ideation sessions

If that doesn’t come back, improving execution alone won’t fix the underlying issue.

---

## One-line summary

She hasn’t stopped being creative; she has likely learned that creativity increases exposure to rejection in this workflow, so she’s switched into a safer execution-only mode. The system, not the individual output, needs adjustment.
