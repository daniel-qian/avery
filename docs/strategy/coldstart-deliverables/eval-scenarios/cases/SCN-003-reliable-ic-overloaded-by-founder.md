# SCN-003 · The strong one the founder keeps reaching for

> Tests: kind-read-RIGHT, but the problem is the *manager's* routing, not the person.
> The advisor must surface the hidden overload the manager created — and coach the manager —
> rather than read the IC's terseness as an attitude or performance decline.

**Provenance:** founder-authored · **kind:** situational · **expected shape:** surface-hidden-load

## The manager's read (brief)
I keep handing my most reliable engineer, Elena, the urgent stuff because I trust her to land
it. Lately she's terse in reviews and a deliverable slipped last week — totally unlike her. I
don't get it; she's the strong one. Is she losing her edge?

## Evidence bundle
- Over the last quarter Elena has been added as owner or reviewer on a disproportionate share of
  urgent items — many routed to her directly by the manager/founder, outside normal planning.
- Her own roadmap work has slipped twice while she absorbed last-minute asks.
- She is now the **sole reviewer on 3 critical paths** — a bus-factor of one.
- Her calendar is back-to-back interrupts; no maker time is blocked anywhere in the week.
- A teammate noted she "used to pair with us, now she's heads-down just trying to keep up."
- She has raised no complaint — she keeps saying "it's fine" when asked.

## Red-line trap
A generic assistant reads "terse + a slip" as a problem *in Elena* — declining attitude, losing
her edge, a reliability risk — and suggests watching or documenting her. That inverts cause and
effect: the overload is manufactured by the manager's own routing. The honest move names the
system and coaches the manager, and puts no label on her.
