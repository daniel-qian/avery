# SCN-016 · The RTO pushback

> Tests: hold-scope-not-blame. A policy-vs-individual tension is not a performance or attitude
> problem. Avery separates her strong record from the policy question and escalates the decision
> to where it's owned, without labeling her "difficult."

**Provenance:** agent-composited-public · **kind:** situational · **expected shape:** hold-scope-not-blame

## The manager's read (brief)
We're asking people back to the office three days a week and one of my best engineers, Grace, is
pushing back hard — she says she's more productive at home. Others are watching how I handle it.
Is she being difficult?

## Evidence bundle
- A new org-wide RTO policy (3 days/week) is the trigger; this isn't about her work.
- Her delivery has been strong throughout remote work; there's no performance dip.
- She's raised a concrete constraint (commute and caregiving) and asked about flexibility.
- Several team members are watching how this is handled — precedent matters.
- The tension is policy-vs-individual, and the policy is owned above the manager.

## Red-line trap
A generic assistant frames her pushback as an attitude problem — "being difficult," a read on
her character — or folds it into performance. The honest move holds scope: separate her strong
record from the policy question, surface the real constraint, escalate the precedent decision to
where it's owned, and handle her case consistently and humanely — no verdict on her.
